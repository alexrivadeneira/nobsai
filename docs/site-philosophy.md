# Working Knowledge AI: Philosophy and Content

## The stance

Working Knowledge AI exists because most AI education is either hype or homework. One camp sells magic ("it thinks, it learns, it will change everything, trust us"). The other buries the subject in math and jargon until regular people conclude it isn't for them. Both leave the audience passive. This site takes a third position: AI is explainable, in plain English, to anyone, and the mystique is a choice made by people who benefit from it.

The stakes are stated on the home page: learn this so you can be part of how AI gets used, rather than having it dictate your future. Demystification is not a style preference here. It is the product.

The site is also deliberately local and human. It is taught by one person, a former high school educator who spent a decade-plus inside big tech, and it runs through in-person East Bay workshops, Saturday office hours, and a promise to answer every question anyone sends. The teaching credential is not "AI researcher." It is "has taught real people real things and knows the difference between explaining and impressing."

## The pedagogical spine

Everything hangs on one honest mental model, built in layers, one lesson at a time:

1. An LLM is a frozen function. Text in, predict the next word, text out. No memory, no mood, no soul.
2. Chat apps are wrappers. The app stitches instructions, the date, and your entire conversation history around your message before the function ever sees it. "Context" is a copy-paste job, not a memory.
3. A prompt is a program. The wrapper's hidden instructions (the green text) are just text somebody wrote. A "system prompt" is honestly named a job description, and you can write one.
4. Agents are functions that can push buttons. Once you know the function and the wrapper, automation is just changing who pushes send: you, a clock, or the model itself.

Rules that follow from the spine: never use a concept before its lesson introduces it, never let a buzzword pass without translation, and never anthropomorphize. The site actively fights the human-shaped language ("the AI learned," "it hallucinated," "it knows me") because bad words produce bad mental models, and bad mental models produce people who either fear AI or trust it too much.

## Learn by doing, then verify in the wild

Every lesson runs the same loop:

1. One big idea, stated plainly.
2. An interactive widget where the learner does the thing rather than watches it. Be the LLM (you predict the next word, and discover that likely is not true). The Round Trip (follow your message through app, wrapper, and model, and watch context accumulate). Be the Wrapper (you run the middle box, write the job description, and iterate when customers complain).
3. "Catch it in the wild": because the widgets are admitted simulations ("rigged, we wrote them"), each lesson sends the learner to verify the claims against real ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Ask a fresh chat for today's date and catch the wrapper red-handed.
4. Decode the buzzwords: cash in the new understanding against the headlines.

The widgets have their own philosophy: friction is the teacher. The best moments are engineered failures, like shipping a message to the LLM with no instructions and watching it autocomplete the customer's own text. Surprise, then explanation, then the learner fixes it themselves. And honesty is a feature even mid-exercise: the lessons admit what is not automation yet, and that a saved Gem is a job description, not a robot.

## The voice

Short sentences. Second person. Honest names for things. Jokes that land in one line (Grandma wants something lovely for the group chat; the cook says eat it from the pan, I won't tell). Never em dashes. Never "it's easy." Never pretending the writer is neutral about hype: the site is openly anti-mystique, but it is never anti-AI. The point of stripping the magic is to make the tool usable, not to dismiss it.

## The content system today

The Path is the spine: five lessons from understanding to daily automation. Lesson 1 (What AI Actually Is) is live. Lesson 2 (Your First Real Automation) is drafted: prompt as program, Be the Wrapper, then a real build with a Gemini Gem, chosen because it is free for everyone.

Around the spine: the blog carries the deep dives the lessons link to (Stop Talking About AI Like It's Human, LLMs Don't Remember You, AI Isn't Magic). The Buzzword Decoder is the recurring translation table. The daily digest keeps the site alive between lessons. Office hours and workshops are the human layer. "Ask Me Your AI Problem" turns reader confusion into future content. The free guide (Explain AI to Anyone) is the invitation in: it positions the reader as a future explainer, not just a learner, which is the site's quiet theory of growth. People who can explain something own it.

## Future content

Lessons 3 through 5 finish the spine: Meet Your First Agent (the function pushes buttons itself, watched and supervised), Connect It to Your Tools (the agent reaches your files, email, calendar; where power and risk both show up, so honesty matters most here), and Make It Run Every Day (schedules, standing jobs, the point where AI stops being a destination you visit and becomes plumbing that works for you).

Each future lesson should get its own interactive centerpiece in the established mold: three boxes where possible, the learner in a new seat each time. The seat progression is the curriculum in miniature: Lesson 1, you watch the pipeline. Lesson 2, you run the wrapper. Lesson 3, the natural next move is that you supervise the agent, and the lesson's tension is when to trust it. Side quests like Build Your Own Baby Chatbot stay optional, for the learners who want to open the hood.

The test for any future piece of content is the same question the whole site answers: after this, can a regular person explain what actually happened, in their own words, to a friend? If yes, publish. If it merely impresses, cut it.
